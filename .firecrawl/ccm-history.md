# Our story

A not-so-brief history of the Center for Cooperative Media, compiled by Joe Amditis.

From the beginning, the underlying assumption of the Center was — and still is — that news organizations can accomplish things by working together that no individual organization would be able to achieve on its own.

Below is a timeline (written and interactive) of major events, achievements, and initiatives by the Center for Cooperative Media from it’s launch in 2012 to the present.

_**NOTE:** This official history of the Center was written and compiled by [Joe Amditis](https://twitter.com/jsamditis), the Center’s associate director of operations (and longest-running staff member), over the course of the last 11 years. For a more detailed look at our work over the last several years, you can [download and read our annual reports here](https://centerforcooperativemedia.org/annual-reports/)._

## **2012: _E pluribus, nuntium_**

We realized, of course, that fostering cooperation among media outfits would be challenging at times. Journalists, by nature, are not always the most cooperative species. Aside from the fierce competition between some of the state’s largest news organizations, there are also rivalries that exist among news organizations of all sizes. Still, we decided to stick a bunch of pins in a map and try to to figure out where the state’s news assets were and find ways that we might be able to get them to play nice.

One of the Center’s biggest advocates and then-associate director, [Debbie Galant](https://medium.com/u/a4f425fb35e5), led the charge as she traveled from city to city, meeting and speaking with newsroom leaders and news entrepreneurs from all over the state, in an attempt to get a better lay of the cooperative media landscape.

Debbie’s first clue that we were on to something came when, while visiting those newsrooms, she met [Justin E. Auciello](https://medium.com/u/3103da68cd97), founder of [Jersey Shore Hurricane News](https://www.facebook.com/JerseyShoreHurricaneNews?ref=br_tf). JSHN was, and still is, a Facebook crowdsourcing phenomenon that became both a model and an inspiration for news entrepreneurs and media startups everywhere.

Then, in late October 2012, Superstorm Sandy hit. Sandy slammed into New Jersey and the rest of the tristate area before the Center even had our website up and running. Yet, in an incredible feat of technology and cooperation, the Center managed to marshal the efforts of eight hyperlocal news sites, plus Jersey Shore Hurricane News, to create [#NJSandy](http://www.scribblelive.com/Event/NJSandy?Page=0), a multi-day live-blog hosted by [ScribbleLive](http://www.scribblelive.com/). The following week, with the added help of [WNYC](http://wnyc.org/), [WHYY](http://whyy.com/) and students of Montclair State’s School of Communication and Media, we follow that with the [#NJVote](http://njnewscommons.org/njvote2/) campaign, which tracked local voting problems in the presidential election in the wake of Sandy’s destruction.

## **2013: Media hacks, micro-grants, and immigration**

After the election, we asked our partners to join forces once again for another editorial project. In the process, we learned that the idea of newsroom collaboration was starting to stick — even without the winds of a natural disaster at our backs. Out of that process came [The Immigration Project](https://www.montclair.edu/school-of-communication-and-media/2013/09/30/11874_nj-news-commons-immigration-project-hits-six-month-mark/), a nine-month effort to report on the lives and experiences of immigrants living in the Garden State.

All of the content produced as part of The Immigration Project was made available to our partners throughout the state using our [Story Exchange](https://letsgather.in/case-studies/how-the-center-for-cooperative-media-built-support-for-collaborative-journalism-in-new-jersey/), an innovative platform that allows news organizations to embed each other’s stories as if they were YouTube videos.

After the new year, we launched two more major initiatives: the Grow & Strengthen program and a series of projects aimed at encouraging investigative and data reporting by members of the NJ news ecosystem.

The main purpose of the [Grow & Strengthen program](https://medium.com/the-engaged-journalism-lab/how-were-helping-build-the-nj-news-ecosystem-e7b134e5b526) was to expand and fortify the news ecosystem in New Jersey. We identified a cohort of news entrepreneurs who were interested in starting new sites, gave them small micro-grants to help get their operations off the ground, and hosted regular coaching sessions at Montclair State University and made sure they connected regularly through monthly peer-to-peer meet-ups. We seeded and nurtured a total of 15 news projects in the state. ( **Disclaimer**: I was leading one of them at the time, called [Muckgers](https://medium.com/u/24e8d842d518).)

[In this post](http://mediashift.org/2015/02/how-the-center-for-cooperative-media-is-fortifying-local-news-in-new-jersey/) from February 2015, our business coach, [Joe Michaud](http://joemichaud.com/), reflected on the qualities that helped make these news startups successful — as well as those that may lead to failure.

Our second major objective was to encourage and support investigative journalism and data reporting by our members. In order to accomplish this, we launched two programs that started in early 2013: [Hack Jersey](http://www.hackjersey.com/2013/03/rounding-up-our-hack-weekend/), our first journalist-coder hackathon; and our [Open Public Records Act](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/let-us-be-your-opra-sherpa/) coaching program.

We partnered with 37-year local government veteran and Bloustein School public policy researcher, [Marc Pfeiffer](http://bloustein.rutgers.edu/pfeiffer/), to provide assistance to investigative reporters and partners looking to file public records requests, free of charge.

## **2014: New Jersey news and a national conference**

With two solid years of cooperative media experimentation under our belts, we started looking outward to find ways we might bring aspects of the New Jersey news ecosystem model — or at least some of the lessons learned from it — to other parts of the country. Part of that process involved hosting a national conference on local media innovation and the future of local news to get a better sense of the national discussion surrounding local journalism as an industry.

In April 2014, we convened more than 250 thought leaders from around the country for a national conference on innovation in local media, called [Innovate Local](http://njnewscommons.org/journalists-gather-to-discuss-and-rebuild-the-business-of-local-news/).

In March, we partnered with the [Center for Community and Ethnic Media](http://ccem.journalism.cuny.edu/) at the [CUNY Graduate School of Journalism](https://www.journalism.cuny.edu/) in New York City to host the first of several [summits for community and ethnic media leaders](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/ethnic-media-in-nj-tickets-10592541577) in the tristate area.

Throughout the year, we continued to coordinate trainings and workshops on topics such as [investigative reporting](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/investigative-reporting-with-ben-lesser-tickets-10594236647#), [starting a news site](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/how-to-start-a-news-site-tickets-10590702075#), [working with spreadsheets](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/working-with-spreadsheets-to-do-investigative-reporting-tickets-11024541701#), and [using your archives](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/archives-re-using-past-content-for-fun-and-profit-tickets-12940831375#) to make money and find new stories.

In May, we hosted the [Open Data NJ Summit](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/open-data-nj-summit-tickets-10825472279#). The goal was to get journalists, watchdogs, citizens activists and government officials to work together to find ways to make state and local data more accessible (and legible).

In November, we held our first [Election Night Open Newsroom](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/pizza-politics-election-night-open-newsroom-tickets-13778023439#) for the 2014 elections. Together with [NJ Spotlight](http://njspotlight.com/), we built and populated a live election map as the results came in on election night. The newsroom was open to all hyperlocal publishers, students, faculty, freelancers, and any journalist without a newsroom. We paid our volunteers in pizza and ended up [winning the 2014 Excellence in Journalism award](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/nj-news-commons-nj-spotlight-together-win-spj-award/) from the [NJ Chapter of the Society of Professional Journalists](http://www.njspj.org/) in the Online Deadline Reporting category.

## **2015: Engagement, new technology and collaborative reporting**

We kicked off 2015 with a push to get some of our partners thinking about the changing mobile news landscape. One of the main goals of our [Conquer Mobile workshop](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/conquer-mobile-mobile-for-news-publishers-tickets-14882549105#) with Montclair State University professor [Amir Husak](http://www.amirhusak.com/), was to help our partners peek under the hood of their smartphone, understand its various components and begin to imagine how it can change their news operation.

We also tried to emphasize the importance of understanding and working with data on a regular basis. In February, we hosted an event called Data Day 2015, which included presentations from representatives of [Parse.ly](http://www.parse.ly/), [Find the Best](http://www.findthebest.com/), [Ethnic NJ](http://ethnicnj.com/), [NJ Advance Media](http://nj.com/), the [Bergen Record](http://northjersey.com/) and the [NJ Data Book](http://njdatabook.rutgers.edu/) at Rutgers University. The goal was to show our partners how data can help them tell stories and how it can help you better understand your community. The event also served as a brainstorming session for the second iteration of our statewide NJ news hackathon, [Hack Jersey 2.0](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/hack-jersey-20-making-journalism-better-through-data-code-and-play-tickets-15412562389#), which we hosted in late March 2015.

Another major theme for the Center and the NJ News Commons in 2015 was community engagement. We wanted to look at all the smart ways media organizations can use engagement to report the news, create brand loyalty, fund their enterprises and turn an active ear towards their communities.

[Engage Local](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/engage-local-national-conference-in-newark-nj-tickets-16693970114#), our second national media conference, kicked off on June 15 at the New Jersey Performing Arts Center in Newark, NJ. The main event consisted of a town hall, during which journalists, civic leaders and the public discussed the issue of redevelopment in New Jersey’s largest city.

The event was part of the [National Community and News Literacy Roundtables Project](http://asne.org/content.asp?pl=15&sl=396&contentid=396), a joint initiative of the [American Society of News Editors](http://asne.org/), [The Robert R. McCormick Foundation](https://www.mccormickfoundation.org/), the [News Literacy Project](http://www.thenewsliteracyproject.org/) and the [American Press Institute](https://www.americanpressinstitute.org/).

Day two of Engage Local was held across the street at the historic Robert Treat Hotel. Highlights included a lunch conversation between [Marty Baron](https://twitter.com/PostBaron), executive editor of [The Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/), and [Merrill Brown](https://twitter.com/merrillbro), then-director of the School of Communication and Media at Montclair State University. [Steve Waldman](https://twitter.com/stevenwaldman?lang=en) also unveiled a proposal to launch the [Report for America](http://reportforamerica.org/) project, which went on to [launch a pilot program to place and pay for three new reporters in the heart of rural Appalachia](https://www.poynter.org/news/report-america-launches-pilot-rural-appalachia).

Collaborative reporting also became a major focus for us in 2015 with the launch of our first statewide reporting collaboration: [Dirty Little Secrets](http://toxicnj.com/): Investigating New Jersey’s Toxic Legacy. The goal of the project was to identify and report on the lesser-known sources of contamination in New Jersey, which is known for its abundance of Superfund sites.

Coordinated by [The Center for Investigative Reporting](http://revealnews.org/), with help from the [Center for Cooperative Media](http://njnewscommons.org/) and the [Rutgers Department of Journalism and Media Studies](http://comminfo.rutgers.edu/journalism-and-media-studies/journalism-and-media-studies-department.html), we set out to explore the local effects of pollution with media partners across New Jersey, including [New Jersey Public Radio/WNYC](http://www.wnyc.org/section/njpr/), [WHYY](http://www.whyy.org/), [NJTV](http://www.njtvonline.org/), [NJ Spotlight](http://www.njspotlight.com/), [Jersey Shore Hurricane News](https://www.facebook.com/JerseyShoreHurricaneNews), [WBGO](https://www.wbgo.org/) and [New Brunswick Today](https://newbrunswicktoday.com/).

Our partners identified more than 15,000 active or pending contaminated sites in the Garden State – that list includes abandoned dry cleaners, concrete companies, tire centers, auto body shop, and lots and lots of gas stations. Most of the sites identified were in some stage of remediation, but we found more than 1,300 with no remediation professional or plan to speak of.

As part of our plan to expand the reach of our partners’ reporting beyond the format of the news story, we partnered with [Storyworks](https://storyworks.revealnews.org/) to translate Brenda Flanagan’s [hard-hitting report for NJTV](https://toxicnj.com/news-story-reaches-new-audiences-as-dark-comedy-ab631e67c0c3) into an hour-long dark comedy called “ [Terra Incognita](http://www.georgestreetplayhouse.org/specialevent/terraincognita)”, performed at the [George Street Playhouse](https://www.georgestreetplayhouse.org/).

We also partnered with WFMU, an independent radio station in Jersey City, to [host a comedy contest series](https://toxicnj.com/turning-toxic-contamination-into-stand-up-comedy-c561b2a329e5) aimed at spotlighting the very serious ways that toxic contamination affects communities across New Jersey through fact-based stand-up comedy.

Unfortunately, Dirty Little Secrets would be the last major project that our then-associate director, Debbie Galant, would work on during her tenure at the Center. Toward the end of the summer, she was diagnosed with breast cancer and chose to step away from her position at the Center to focus on her treatment (and a podcast she started with her son as a way to chronicle her battle with cancer, which they dubbed [The Chemo Files](https://soundcloud.com/chemo-files)). Galant would go on to make a full recovery and is currently in remission — plus, The Chemo Files [won the 2016 June L. Biedler Prize for cancer reporting](http://baristanet.com/2016/04/the-chemo-files-podcast-wins-award-for-cancer-reporting/)!

With Debbie gone, the day-to-day operations of the Center were left for me to handle while the university continued the search for a new director and associate director. In the meantime, I was temporarily promoted to acting director and acting associate director.

## **2016: New leadership, new projects, and new collaborations**

We kicked off 2016 with [a crowdfunding campaign](http://njnewscommons.org/were-launching-another-collaborative-reporting-project-join-us/) to raise at least $30,000 to support collaborative reporting on immigration issues and policies in New Jersey. We called the project [In the Shadow of Liberty: Immigration in New Jersey](http://shadowofliberty.org/). We partnered with [NJ Spotlight](http://njspotlight.com/) to raise the money, two-thirds of which would be dedicated to their own reporting efforts over the course of the next year.

The crowdfunding campaign, hosted on the now-defunct [Beaconreader platform](https://www.poynter.org/news/beacon-reader-journalism-crowdfunding-platform-closing-down), was a resounding success. We managed to raise more than $31,000 to support the collaborative project, and we immediately [began looking for reporting partners](http://njnewscommons.org/center-for-cooperative-media-to-award-8500-in-immigration-reporting-grants-apply-now/).

We received a range of grant applications from journalists and local publishers across the state. In order to ensure diversity in our grantee selection process, we also partnered with the [now-defunct](http://newamericamedia.org/2017/11/pacific-news-service-dba-new-america-media-announces-closure-of-organization.php) [New America Media](http://newamericamedia.org/) to help us attract ethnic, community, and transnational media grantees. In the end, [we awarded more than $5,800 in reporting grants](http://njnewscommons.org/center-for-cooperative-media-awards-5800-in-immigration-reporting-grants/) to nine local reporting outlets and journalists. We also awarded $1,000 to Anthony Ewing of [EthnicNJ.com](http://ethnicnj.com/) to help him update his maps of ethnic and immigrant-owned restaurants and business in New Jersey. Ewing also used part of the money to create [a list of available state and local immigration resources](http://ethnicnj.com/immigrant-nj/).

In total, our partners produced at least 39 stories, focusing on more than 12 different local and ethnic communities, as part of the [Shadow of Liberty collaboration](http://shadowofliberty.org/).

**_The Center gets a new director_** Stefanie Murray was [named as the new director](https://www.montclair.edu/newscenter/2016/04/29/16383_stefanie-murray-named-director-of-the-center-for-cooperative-media-at-montclair-state-university/) of the Center in April, and she immediately set out to familiarize herself with our partners and the individual issues they faced. Murray’s long tenure as a reporter and editor in newsrooms across the country meant she already had a strong grasp on the most glaring issue facing publishers and reporters alike: revenue.

**_Money, money, money_** With that in mind, we decided to host two sustainability summits for NJ News Commons members — [one in North Jersey](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/north-jersey-sustain-local-summit-for-digital-publishers-tickets-25438817187#) and [one in South Jersey](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/south-jersey-sustain-local-summit-for-digital-publishers-tickets-25357649412#) — in preparation for our annual national media conference, [Sustain Local](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/sustain-local-2016-national-conference-tickets-26519869644#), which was scheduled for the fall. The turnout at both events was impressive; it was clear that this was something our partners cared about and wanted to discuss further.

In the meantime, we were already busy soliciting applications for the second iteration of our [Sales and Revenue Bootcamp](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/sales-revenue-bootcamp-tickets-18780923247), which first launched in the summer of 2015. Business coach Joe Michaud and sales guru Eleanor Cippel worked with a handful of partners and their prospective sales representatives to hone their skills and become acquainted with what Cippel calls the “ [9 Week Intentional Seller Program](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/center-scratchings-sales-revenue-bootcamp/).”

Revenue and sustainability were obviously the two of the biggest themes that year, but there was another aspect of journalism’s downturn over the last decade or two that remained largely unaddressed: layoffs and newsroom job losses. Stefanie Murray had seen this play out first-hand in newsrooms where she worked and, after news broke in September about [another round of layoffs in Gannett newsrooms across the state](http://www.nj.com/bergen/index.ssf/2016/09/gannett_announces_layoffs_at_the_record_2_month_af.html), we were determined to do something — _anything_ — to address the issue in New Jersey.

With support from the Geraldine R. Dodge Foundation, the Center’s research director [Sarah Stonbely](https://twitter.com/SarahStonbely) began working on a report that aimed to examine the effects these newsroom layoffs were having on the coverage of the communities that lost their reporters. The paper, which was released in March 2017, was titled “ [Layoffs in Local Newsrooms: Documenting the changing New Jersey local journalism ecosystem, 2016–2017.](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/03/LayoffsinLocalNewsroomsFinal-1.pdf)”

In the meantime, the Center hosted a free luncheon, networking event and roundtable discussion at Montclair State University. We called the event “ [Life After the Newsroom](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/life-after-the-newsroom-tickets-29307407244#),” and it featured a panel of people who were either let go from legacy organizations or took a buyout, and went on to forge new or different career paths for themselves in the aftermath. The event was completely sold out by the time the doors opened.

## **2017: One nation, under collaboration**

It’s been a crazy year for the Center — and for many others, I’m sure — but 2017 has also been one of our most productive and promising years yet.

The theme of this year was, of course, _collaboration, collaboration, collaboration_. Whether we’re talking about our international [Collaborative Journalism Summit](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/collaborativej-wrap-up-links-and-resources-from-the-collaborative-journalism-summit/) in May, our [third consecutive statewide collaborative reporting](http://votingblocknj.com/) on the governor’s race (including our [collaborative Election Night coverage](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/nj-news-commons-nj-spotlight-election-night-open-newsroom-grows-deepens-partnership-with-5d01544186be)), our [latest report identifying six different collaborative reporting models](https://collaborativejournalism.org/models), our [seven Rita Allen collaborative reporting grantees](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/center-cooperative-media-announces-six-winners-collaborative-reporting-project-open-call/), or the partnerships we’ve formed in recent months with newsrooms across the country [as part of the Facebook Journalism Project](https://media.fb.com/2017/01/11/facebook-journalism-project/) — in 2017, collaboration was king.

**_New research, new programs, and a new building_** One of our crowning achievements this year was the release of our second major research project: [Comparing Models of Collaborative Journalism](https://collaborativejournalism.org/models/). In September, research director Sarah Stonbely unveiled her findings during the grand opening of the university’s new state-of-the-art School of Communication and Media building, which boasts [the first end-to-end 4K broadcast production studio](https://www.commercialintegrator.com/av/video/sony-pro-montclair-state-university-4k-studio/) and control room at any university in the country.

Stonbely’s research identifies six models of collaborative journalism currently being used for news collaborations across the world. The report also cites 44 ongoing collaborations, which include more than 500 newsrooms and $200 million in funding for collaborations since the early 2000s.

**_A bigger table with different voices_**

As with all of our projects, however, diversity is paramount to success. The Center has worked consistently over the last five years to improve in this area, and we still have much work to do. That is why we started this year by partnering again with New America Media — which boasts a nationwide network of ethnic and diaspora media partners — and NJ Advance Media to [host a roundtable discussion in January with representatives from New Jersey’s ethnic press](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/new-america-media-roundtable-issues-facing-nj-ethnic-media-in-covering-trump-tickets-31073915919#). The goal of this meeting was to identify the editorial and other priorities of their respective organizations and communities.

**_The Collaborative Journalism Database is born_** One of the Center’s crowning achievements in 2018 from an organizational perspective was the launch of the [Collaborative Journalism Database](https://collaborativejournalism.org/database-search-sort-learn-collaborative-projects-around-world/), a sprawling compendium of collaborative reporting projects and networks for other reporters and newsrooms to peruse and learn from.

Since the beginning of that year, the Center had been collecting, organizing and standardizing information about dozens of collaborative journalism projects around the world. Our goal was to maintain a database that can serve as a hub of information about collaborative journalism, something that would be useful to journalists, scholars, media executives, funders and others seeking information on how such projects work, who’s doing them and what they’re covering.

Toward the end of the year, the database would be [translated into both Spanish and Portuguese](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/collaborative-journalism-database-expands-to-include-translations-in-spanish-portuguese-922192ad3c08).

_**Understanding how New Jersey gets (and feels about) its local news, plus ecosystem mapping**_

Local news outlets in 2018 were still competing for the attention of their audiences like never before. In such a high-choice environment, the Center wanted to learn more about what local news providers could do to make sure their news is valuable to their audiences. Likewise, what kinds of data do organizations with missions to support and strengthen local news need to do their work? Dr. Stonbely’s report, “ [What do New Jersey news consumers want? Assessing satisfaction with local news](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/CCM-Free-Press-survey-analysis-FINAL.pdf),” laid some of the groundwork for answering these questions.

Later that year, Stonbely and co-author Prof. Tara George set their sights on understanding the influence of three commonly-cited features of success when it comes to digital local news outlets: the background of the publisher, experimentation with different revenue streams, and the wealth of the surrounding community. Their report, titled “ [Health and wealth in local news: Examining community wealth, publisher background, source of revenue](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/01/Health-and-Wealth-at-Local-News-Outlets-10-1-18-final.pdf),” found that of the three, only the wealth of the community is statistically correlated with “success” (which we define as organizational longevity + three indicators of economic success).

Finally, the Center believes that knowing the true landscape of the local news ecosystem in this digital age could help our work, and others, in so many ways. More than a year earlier, Stonbely began working on a new method to address this urgent question.

Stonbely had been considering the work a three-phase process. [Phase 1](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/12/Mapping-Local-News-Ecosystems-Phase1-1-23-19-1.pdf), now complete, was a literature review that summarized the entire ecosystem/ecology literature to-date, organized the studies according to eight different typologies, and fleshed out a new method that will allow for both depth and scale in the study of local news ecosystems. Phase 1 was co-authored by Sarah Stonbely, Magda Konieczna, assistant professor of journalism at Temple University, and Jesse Holcomb, assistant professor of communication arts and sciences at Calvin College.

**_Collaborating with community_** Community engagement was another big theme for the Center in 2018, and it played a large role in several of our major projects that year. The big two were Voting Block and Stories of Atlantic City, both of which relied heavily on the philosophy of the social and engagement journalism schools of practice.

Voting Block 2018 came on the heels of an historic election in New Jersey. The previous year, New Jersey chose a governor without an incumbent in the race for the first time in 12 years. It was a critical moment for the future of the state, and it came at a time when conversations across the political divide were increasingly difficult. That year, 25 newsrooms that cover New Jersey joined the collaborative reporting initiative and pioneered a new way to cover elections that brought together newsrooms to use the same engagement framework to inform their reporting. The idea was to spark political dialogue in New Jersey, amplify local priorities from the public for the next governor’s agenda, and deepen engagement between communities and newsrooms.

Coordinated by The Center for Cooperative Media, [Reveal from the Center for Investigative Reporting](https://revealnews.org/) and [New America Media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_America_Media), the project brought together a diverse cohort of media organizations, including WNYC, WHYY, NJ Spotlight, The Record, Route 40, Zaman Amerika and Reporte Hispano, to collectively pilot this reporting method. The Geraldine R. Dodge Foundation provided critical funding to support the project.

In the end, [Voting Block](https://www.votingblocknj.com/) newsrooms talked with more than 100 neighbors about their political priorities, produced over 70 local stories and together provided statewide coverage of the election as a network. The project was [so successful and so well-received](https://revealnews.org/blog/3-lessons-we-learned-from-voting-block-nj/), that we quickly agreed to launch a third iteration of the project the following year in 2019, even though there were no major elections scheduled for that cycle.

That same spirit of community engagement and participatory journalism is what fueled our work in the southern half of the state with the launch of [Stories of Atlantic City](https://storiesofatlanticcity.com/). The term refers to journalism or storytelling that highlights the assets of a particular individual or community instead of the deficits and shortcomings.

The first phase of the project culminated in the publication of eight stories and a community launch party later in May 2019. The results, highlighted in [this report](https://storiesofatlanticcity.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/9/2019/10/Stories-of-AC-report-07NOV2019.pdf), were powerful and the founding partners agreed to continue the effort in Atlantic City and to take the concept to other communities across the U.S. in future iterations.

_**Travel the country and learn from your peers**_

**_Mapping New Jersey’s local news ecosystem (cont’d)_**

In March, Dr. Sarah Stonbely released Phase II of her ongoing ecosystem mapping project with researcher Jesse Holcomb. The goal of the project was to create the most comprehensive, but scalable, local news ecosystem mapping project to-date, one that could be replicated across the United States. It buildt explicitly on the methodology and execution of Phil Napoli’s [News Measures Research Project](https://dewitt.sanford.duke.edu/news-measures-research-project/), but with some important tweaks.

In her [first post](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/local-news-ecosystem-mapping-balancing-depth-vs-scale-8841da2199b1) about the mapping project, Stonbely discussed the balance between depth and scale in news ecosystem studies. [Here](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/local-news-ecosystem-mapping-using-databases-to-build-a-comprehensive-list-of-local-news-producers-f33bac5da65b) she talked about the use of databases to build a “census” of local news providers for a given area.

Understanding any local news ecosystem is impossible without first understanding the depth and breadth of news production there. It sounds simple, but the reality of answering that question with data is much more complex. Obviously, producers of local news operate in several media: print, broadcast, and online. Failing to account for any one medium will produce an inaccurate picture of the local news landscape in question. The problem was that no comprehensive list or database existed that covered local news producers across all media.

**_Stories of Atlantic City goes live_**

In Atlantic City, we saw an opportunity to test out a new kind of collaborative journalism effort, one that focused entirely on restorative narrative stories and one that put the community squarely in the driver’s seat of what stories got told. We decided the call it [Stories of Atlantic City](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/stories-of-atlantic-city-launches-pairing-community-members-with-media-in-a-unique-new-a0360154819a). The partnership began in fall of 2018 when we convened a meeting of journalists and community members to discuss media coverage in Atlantic City and consider what stories would look like here if they were told through a restorative narrative lens.

The basic premise is simple: A group of community members are going to find good stories, and a group of media outlets have agreed to tell those stories.

At its core, SoAC is a collaborative [restorative narratives](https://ivoh.org/what-we-do/restorative-narrative/) series. It’s an initiative that grew out of [Free Press](https://www.freepress.net/)’ [News Voices](https://www.freepress.net/issues/future-journalism/local-journalism) work in Atlantic City in 2015 and culminated in a [partnership in 2018](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/using-restorative-narrative-to-tell-the-story-of-atlantic-city-efb88b4a6e66) between Free Press, the Center for Cooperative Media at Montclair State University, Stockton University, [Images and Voices of Hope (ivoh)](http://ivoh.org/), a group of engaged community members and five local media outlets.

The team and a group of community members reconvened in April with our media partners, including [The Press of Atlantic City](https://www.pressofatlanticcity.com/), [Route 40](http://rtforty.com/), Atlantic City Times, [Breaking AC](http://breakingac.com/), [Stockton University](https://stockton.edu/) and [SNJ Today](http://snjtoday.com/), to pitch the stories. At the end of that discussion the journalists each chose a story to tell.

Our media partners, working with Ilsa Flanagan of ivoh and [Stefanie Murray](https://twitter.com/stefaniemurray) and [Joe Amditis](https://twitter.com/jsamditis) at the Center, then [reported out those stories](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/stories-of-atlantic-city-publishes-with-tales-of-success-overcoming-hardship-and-resilience-57aae5ab43db) and co-published or co-broadcasted them all on an agreed-upon day in May. Following publication, we hosted [a storytellers event](https://www.facebook.com/events/447733189103809/) in Atlantic City to bring the community together and let the people featured in the pieces talk about their experiences in front of a live audience.

In November, we released a detailed report covering the major challenges and successes of the project, which you can [click here to read in full](https://storiesofatlanticcity.com/report/).

_**Studying the state of ethnic and community media in New Jersey**_

Ethnic and community media are crucial interlocutors in the many ethnic, religious, and non-English-language communities in New Jersey and beyond. Yet, as with the local news landscape in general, detailed understanding of the numerous providers in the area is spotty.

The Center’s report, titled “ [The State of Ethnic and Community Media in New Jersey](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/state-of-ethnic-media-in-new-jersey-new-report-documents-a-robust-yet-under-appreciated-sector-2fef8ca9bdd0),” was our first attempt at putting together a detailed accounting of the ethnic and community media providers serving the Garden State. In addition to a “census” of outlets, the report includes both quantitative and qualitative description of their operations, a discussion of themes common among them, and recommendations for how we and others might support them as they continue to serve as crucial news and information providers to their respective communities.

In the report, Sarah Stonbely and Oni Advincula documented a robust and optimistic, yet under-resourced and under-appreciated sector made up of at least 119 outlets serving New Jersey. These media outlets are generally well established with roots in communities dating back decades. Stonbely and Advincula also found that they tend to be close to their audiences, drawing on voices and viewpoints from a wide variety of community members and taking a high degree of input from them as well.

You can [read the full report here](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/state_of_ethnic_media.pdf).

In addition to the report, we created a [database of ethnic and community outlets](http://bit.ly/njethnicmediadatabase) that serve New Jersey and [a submission form](http://bit.ly/njethnicoutletsuggest) where any ethnic and community outlet not included in our database can be identified for inclusion. The database includes outlet names as well as information such as community served, whether the outlet provides local news, and language of publication

_**A year-long campus climate collaboration**_ Following the launch of the NJ College News Commons at the end of 2018, student reporters, editors, and advisers from six colleges and universities from across New Jersey — Montclair State University, New Jersey Institute of Technology, The College of New Jersey, Fairleigh Dickinson University, Rowan University, and New Jersey City University — came together to launch [a year-long climate collaboration project](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/nj-college-news-commons-launches-semester-long-collaborative-climate-reporting-project-72c2224e9fda) starting in the fall of 2019. The goal of the project was to bring together as many campus media organizations in the state as possible and help them to work together on reporting on climate and environment stories over the course of the following two semesters.

The project began in earnest earlier that year after students from multiple campus media organizations independently decided to cover the climate crisis during the fall 2019 semester. Since then, we discussed the idea with the rest of the NJCNC partners over the course of a few months, and [decided to host a climate change reporting trip](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/nj-college-news-commons-launches-semester-long-collaborative-climate-reporting-project-72c2224e9fda) at River Barge Park along the banks of the Hackensack River on Aug. 23. The event served as the official kickoff for the project.

The students heard from New York Times environment reporter [John Schwartz](https://www.nytimes.com/by/john-schwartz), ProPublica engagement reporter [Maya Miller](https://twitter.com/mayatmiller?lang=en), and NJ Advance Media’s environment and outdoors reporter [Michael Sol Warren](https://twitter.com/MSolDub) about what it’s like to do this kind of work on a daily basis. We discussed everything from the language we use when describing people and their beliefs about climate to the kinds of issues students should expect to face when attempting to report on climate-related issues on campus and in their respective communities.

After the roundtable was over, we joined boat captains from the New Jersey Sports and Exhibition Authority (formerly the Meadowlands Commission) and a representative from the NY/NJ Baykeeper for an hour-long pontoon boat ride up and down the Hackensack River to learn more about New Jersey’s Meadowlands and the creatures that inhabit them.

Everyone in our collaborative agreed to make climate and environmental coverage a central part of their work over the course of the fall semester. Which stories our partners ran, how many stories they ran, and what those stories said was completely up to each partner — each outlet retained its editorial independence.

## **2020: The world grinds to a halt**

The beginning of 2020 at the Center began like any other year: we were busy announcing our upcoming training schedule, judging submissions for that year’s [Excellence in Local News Awards](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/announcing-the-winners-of-the-2020-excellence-in-local-news-awards-4d40e1e0fd5c), and pretending like we still had plenty of time to prepare for the upcoming [2020 Collaborative Journalism Summit](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLgq6gq6KYlKkeNEtHkzaJzEz5qq6PMB6P). Little did we know what this year would have in store for us, for New Jersey, for journalism, and for the world.

**_New Jersey local news gets a shot in the arm (pun intended)_** Before things started getting dark, however, the local journalism scene in the Garden State was treated to at least one bit of good news: the state government finally [released the funding](https://www.freepress.net/news/press-releases/new-jersey-releases-funds-innovative-initiative-support-civic-minded-news-and) for the first-in-the-nation public funding experiment, the [NJ Civic Information Consortium](https://njcivicinfo.org/). The idea for the Consortium came from the folks over at Free Press, particularly the minds of Fiona Morgan and the execution of Mike Rispoli, and the bill had initially [passed in 2018 to great fanfare](https://www.nytimes.com/2018/07/30/nyregion/nj-legislature-community-journalism.html?smid=tw-nytimes&smtyp=cur) (and the usual [hand-wringing](https://www.cjr.org/the_media_today/government-funding-journalism.php)).

Now, the state had finally released the first $1 million of the $2 million that had been earmarked for the Consortium. “The bill establishing the consortium passed the state legislature with overwhelming bipartisan support in 2018 and was signed into law by Gov. Murphy,” [according to Free Press](https://www.freepress.net/news/press-releases/new-jersey-releases-funds-innovative-initiative-support-civic-minded-news-and). “But while the state’s FY 2019 budget dedicated money for the consortium, Murphy later said that the earmarked funds were not yet available. The Murphy administration released this money on Thursday.”

What’s more, Montclair State University would later be [chosen to serve as the host university](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/nj-civic-information-consortium-now-accepting-grant-applications-for-first-round-of-funding-f1bcee4b3f45) for the Consortium, with the Center providing invaluable back office and logistical services to the board during the first round of grants.

_**Counting the historically undercounted**_

During those first few months of the year, when everyone could still meet in person, the Center was focused on its [NJ Media Counts](https://collaborativejournalism.org/njmediacounts/) project, an attempt to increase coverage of and turnout for the upcoming 2020 Census in New Jersey’s marginalized and most vulnerable communities. Project coordinator Oni Advincula and I were working hard to coordinate a [series of town hall discussions and community meetings](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/center-for-cooperative-media-launches-2020-census-reporting-initiative-in-nj-communities-of-color-4312f7aa9094) in New Jersey’s Turkish, Black, Latino, Indian and South Asian, Korean, and other communities across the state.

The first few town halls went off without a hitch, and we were able to gather members of various underserved communities in a room together, share a meal, and discuss the importance of the Census in the hopes that attendees — and the ethnic and community media reporters in the room — would take that information and enthusiasm back to their families and friends.

_**Supporting collaborative news ecosystems**_

The final stretch of the Peer Learning + Collaboration Fund involved conducting information assessments in three NJ cities, as mentioned above, and a series of “Share + Learn” events with different newsrooms across the country. In Newark, we convened a group of community stakeholders and local news and information providers on March 19 for a [virtual workshop](https://collaborativejournalism.org/newarkworkshop/) on how to build a healthy, collaborative news ecosystem in New Jersey’s largest and most diverse city.

As the Center had been seeing through the early findings of its news ecosystem mapping project, led by our research director Sarah Stonbely, there are (growing) news deserts in South Jersey. In particular, we were concerned about the (growing) lack of news and information provided by and for communities of color.

So we decided to [team up with PABJ and see what we could do](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/center-for-cooperative-media-pabj-launch-south-jersey-information-equity-project-bb74d330a0c5) to help out. Hence, the [South Jersey Information Equity Project](https://sjiep.org/) was born. The project’s goal is to increase the amount of credible news and information produced by and for communities of color in South Jersey. In late June, the SJIEP officially began when we [hired Sarah Glover — veteran journalist, leadership fellow with Press Forward, and former PABJ president — to lead the first phase of the project](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/south-jersey-information-equity-project-launches-works-to-examine-media-inequity-d2d4d699a031), which included research into the current information ecosystem in South Jersey paired with an assessment of information need gaps.

In April, we hosted the first virtual [“Share + Learn” event of 2020 in partnership with The Devil Strip](https://youtu.be/xKMezvqpwwk), which was based out of Akron, Ohio. The event was also supported by the Community Information Cooperative. This session featured a discussion with staff, contributors, and some of the nearly 400 community members who had become co-owners of The Devil Strip about the value of shared governance and ownership.

Toward the end of the month, Sarah Stonbely and Jesse Holcomb released the first phase of their [News Ecosystem Mapping Project](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/we-mapped-the-local-news-providers-for-all-of-new-jersey-to-see-the-topography-of-the-local-news-1838272b82d5), which identified every local news and information provider in the state. This study built on the work of others in the field in two key ways: first, by identifying the complete range of journalistic news providers (print, digital and broadcast) serving an entire US state, and second, by mapping those news providers according to the communities they cover, rather than where they are headquartered, as some have done.

Their research yielded a final tally of 779 — a significant number, but one that is difficult to compare because, to their knowledge, no other state has a comparable list. Their hope is that this research can serve as a proof of concept that can be replicated elsewhere. That list of 779 outlets include news providers from all media — newspapers, digital-first or digital-native online outlets, radio stations, and television stations. Included within that number are university newspapers, outlets that primarily serve ethnic communities, and religious outlets that provide timely news and information about those important institutions.

Stonbely and Holcomb also gathered information such as outlets’ websites (if any), ownership structures, and coverage area as reported by the outlet. [Read more about the project and see the full map here.](https://newsecosystems.org/)

_**Remembering all those we loved and lost**_

By the middle of June 2020, at least [12,835 people](https://www.google.com/search?q=total+nj+deaths+covid&rlz=1C1CHBF_enUS899US899&oq=total+nj+deaths&aqs=chrome.2.0j69i57j0l2.3071j0j1&sourceid=chrome&ie=UTF-8) had died in New Jersey due to COVID-19.

We had to revise that sentence several times since Stefanie Murray first started writing [the post announcing](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/loved-and-lost-statewide-media-collaboration-launched-to-identify-remember-nj-covid-19-victims-193a3edb43e3) the launch of our new collaboration with NorthJersey.com, and that number has since ballooned to [28,177 deaths](https://www.google.com/search?client=firefox-b-1-d&q=nj+coronavirus+dashboard) as I’m writing this history in November 2021.

Earlier that year, journalists at The Record and NorthJersey.com started [a project to memorialize New Jersey’s COVID-19 victims](https://www.northjersey.com/story/news/coronavirus/2020/03/31/lost-and-loved-nj-family-friends-taken-us-coronavirus/5091229002/). At that time, the state had several hundred deaths. Stefanie reached out to the top editor there, [Dan Sforza](https://twitter.com/SforzaDan), and by the time they got on the phone the death toll had hit 1,500 and was growing rapidly.

Stefanie’s ask was simple: Given the scale of the tragedy, would The Record and NorthJersey.com be willing to share management of its project with us at the Center for Cooperative Media, and allow us to transform it into a statewide collaboration? Yes, Dan said. He and his team knew they wouldn’t be able to tackle the project alone. And that’s become ever more true as the number of deaths has swelled.

Thus, the [Loved + Lost project](https://lovedandlostnj.com/) was re-born as a statewide collaboration, thanks to initial support from the Nicholson Foundation. Our goal was two-fold: First, to name as many New Jersey COVID-19 victims as we were able to verify and add them to our wall of names at [LovedAndLostNJ.com](http://lovedandlostnj.com/); and second, to have journalists and storytellers do as many stories as possible about the people who died.

In September 2020, Center staff [presented the project during a brief session](https://youtu.be/EvMJ4hxUfDM) at the 2020 Online News Association conference.

If you have lost a loved one to COVID-19 and would like to add their name to the wall of names, you can still [use this form to send us that information](https://forms.gle/zXzsnc2YaB25oxC46).

_**More research and reports released**_

Since climate change is (and will continue to be) one of the biggest stories of most people’s lifetimes, we decided to take a look at how journalists are working together to tackle the topic and all of its related issues. The result is a research paper by Caroline Porter we released in July 2020, titled “ [Adapting to a changing climate: How collaboration addresses unique challenges in climate-change and environmental reporting.](https://collaborativejournalism.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/8/2020/07/climate-change-caroline-porter-july-16-2020.pdf)” The report is part of a series of research and guides related to collaborative journalism produced in 2020 by the Center, thanks to generous support from Rita Allen Foundation.

In August, Impact Architects, together with the Center for Cooperative Media, released a research project (which you can [read and download in full here](https://collaborativejournalism.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/8/2020/07/Collaborating-for-Change-Approaches-to-measuring-the-impact-of-collaborative-journalism-31JUL2020.pdf)) that was conducted over the course of the previous three years to understand what type of impact collaborative journalism initiatives aimed for, and how they knew if they succeeded. This research was funded in part by Rita Allen Foundation.

In September, the Center published the culmination of more than a year and a half of internal assessments and strategic planning processes by articulating our core values and six strategic objectives for our future work. [Read this post](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/center-for-cooperative-media-articulates-its-core-values-6-strategic-objectives-for-future-work-d9ce450eeb46) and you’ll find the Center’s mission, vision, values and strategic objectives for future work over the next few years. This reflects months of thought and planning, and is only the tip of the iceberg. For each objective, we’ve got early plans in place for execution, which we will revisit as a team at least quarterly to assess and evaluate.

In October, the Center released a full report on the successes of its [NJ Media Counts](https://collaborativejournalism.org/njmediacounts/) initiative, which provided support for 12 reporters and editors from New Jersey’s ethnic media to produce in-depth stories about the challenges and opportunities of the 2020 Census in diverse communities — [Gujarati](http://gujaratdarpan.com/ts082020/), [Turkish](http://zamanamerika.com/haberler/gundem/nufus-sayimina-az-sure-kaldi-ancak-njdeki-turk-toplumunun-karsilastigi-bazi-zorluklar-devam-ediyor/), [African American](https://pressofatlanticcity.com/news/atlantic-city-discussion-about-the-census/article_3e09438f-028b-5296-a796-0d948db27103.html#1), [Hindi](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=km6J1W45Jzc&feature=youtu.be&list=PLgq6gq6KYlKk6sTd4RH9J43AA5EgohtLt), [Hungarian](https://nepszava.us/a-nepszamlasasi-kerdezobiztosoknak-biztonsagosan-ajtot-nyithatnak-az-amerikai-magyarok/), [Bangladeshi](https://m.facebook.com/story.php?story_fbid=899367843852903&id=703398296358165&_rdr), [Pakistani](https://thepakistaninewspaper.com/census-2020-new-jerseys-pakistani-community-undeterred-by-covid-19-lockdown/), [Filipino](https://balitangamerica.tv/u-s-census-bureau-extends-deadline-for-self-response-forms-to-october/), [Chinese](https://collaborativejournalism.org/2020/02/03/satellite-babies-in-new-jerseys-chinese-community-are-most-at-risk-of-being-undercounted-in-2020-census/) and [Latino](https://reportehispano.com/2020/08/organizaciones-intensifican-campana-del-censo-en-new-brunswick-ante-baja-participacion/). _**Electing a new president in the middle of a pandemic**_

As if nationwide lockdowns, racial justice uprisings, and a global pandemic weren’t enough, 2020 was also a presidential election year. It was time for America to decide whether to stick with another four years of Donald J. Trump, or bring his reign to one-term end.

The 2020 U.S. elections came at a time of deep partisan divide amid a globalpandemic and a painful national reckoning with racism. Misinformation anddisinformation coursed through social media platforms. The stakes could nothave been higher.

The pandemic dramatically changed the way politicians campaigned and howpeople voted, adding more stress to an already chaotic-feeling time. Newsorganizations around the country focused much more attention on the votingprocess in 2020, as mail-in ballots became commonplace in many states. InNew Jersey, ​it was the first time​ that every registered voter was sent amail-in ballot.While coverage of down-ballot races and ballot questions were important tostatewide and local news organizations, the presidential electionovershadowed many other things.Security was a big concern, as well — the security of the election itself, andsecurity for journalists who would be covering it.

In surveying the landscape and talking with partners, the Center decided to put early emphasis on helping news organizations understand and explain the voting process and on providing additional training and incentivizing ethnic media and mainstream media collaboration. Then in late fall, content sharing was added to the mix with the launch of Votebeat.

Among the 2020 election initiatives the Center spearheaded were:

— An arrangement with NJ Spotlight News to make its statewide “How to vote in New Jersey” story and video about mail-in ballots available for republication; social graphics about the voting process were also created and shared with NJ News Commons members.

— Translation of the NJ Spotlight News “How to vote in New Jersey” piece from English into an additional 10 languages; the article was then published by 10 ethnic media outlets.

— Creation of a pop-up newsletter to share content produced by Votebeat with NJ News Commons members for republication.

— Fellowships with five ethnic and community media reporters to allow them to do additional election-related reporting.

— Telebriefings and training webinars, including one about disaster planning with Election SOS.

— The provision of legal help on and after Election Day for journalists facing legal issues regarding reporting at polling stations or covering protests or celebrations.

## **2021: There it is again, that funny feeling**

As I write this, we’re coming up on the second anniversary of the start of the global coronavirus pandemic and even though we have multiple effective and available vaccines — we might even have one in pill form soon — people are still dying by the dozens every day and hospitals are still struggling to cope with the influx of COVID patients. There’s a sort of perpetual unease and background anxiety that permeates society and serves as the backdrop for all social interactions, especially as people and organizations timidly attempt to start holding large public gatherings again. That [funny feeling](https://youtu.be/WPB6u1BqZqU), if you will, doesn’t seem like it’s going away [any time soon](https://www.northjersey.com/story/news/coronavirus/2021/12/05/first-nj-omicron-covid-variant-case-plane/8879603002/). Yet, there is still work to do, and the Center is still determined to do it.

_**Vaccines are available, but some remain hesitant**_

The FDA first approved the Pfizer vaccine under an [emergency use authorization](https://www.dicardiology.com/article/fda-clears-first-covid-19-vaccine-under-emergency-use-authorization) in mid-December 2020, but the rollout process was anything but smooth. Many communities across the United States, [especially communities of color](https://www.theguardian.com/society/2021/aug/20/black-americans-covid-19-vaccine-hesitancy-mistrust), were immediately hesitant to get vaccinated — some communities for better reasons than others.

As such, one of the Center’s first actions of 2021 was to schedule [press briefings about the COVID-19 vaccine rollout](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/vaccine-telebriefing-for-new-jersey-journalists-tickets-137668735895) and response in New Jersey. The press briefings were exclusively for NJ News Commons members, particularly our ethnic and community media partners, with the goal of ensuring our partners had direct access to medical and public health experts so they could provide their communities with the latest accurate information about the vaccine.

Our second COVID-19 press briefing specifically focused on the [racial disparities and potential solutions regarding vaccine distribution](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/racial-disparities-in-vaccine-distribution-briefing-for-nj-journalists-tickets-141871339997) in New Jersey. Speakers included Amanda Medina-Forrester, executive director of the Office of Minority and Multicultural Health at the NJ Department of Health, Gregory Paulson, executive director of the Trenton Health Team, Dr. Stephanie Silvera, epidemiologist and professor in the Public Health Department at Montclair State University, and Rev. Charles Boyer, founding director of Salvation and Social Justice.

Our third COVID-19 press briefing focused on [how our partners can spot mis- and disinformation about the vaccine](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/vaccine-mis-and-disinformation-telebriefing-for-nj-journalists-tickets-145772281819), how to properly report on it, and how to provide factual information to keep their respective communities informed.

Following [our last COVID-19 reporting fellowship](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/covid19fellows/) in 2020, the Center launched [another fellowship for ethnic and community media](https://centerforcoop.cdn-pi.com/post-covid-fellows/) in New Jersey. This latest fellowship, apocryphally titled “After the Pandemic: Reporting on NJ underserved communities,” was launched to support up to 10 journalists from New Jersey’s community and ethnic press for a total of six months. The Center hosted monthly press briefings for the fellows on various topics of timely interest and general relevance, and we provided access to experts and public officials in the state. Oni Advincula coordinated the fellowship program, and helped to facilitate translation services and disseminate fact sheets. Each fellow received stipends of $2,000 and opportunities for additional reporting partnerships with large mainstream New Jersey media organizations.

In July, we hosted [our fourth COVID-19 press briefing](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/press-event-tracking-the-delta-variant-in-new-jersey-tickets-164171305795) for NJ News Commons members and COVID reporting fellows. This time we brought together experts and public health officials as cases and hospitalizations were skyrocketing throughout the state due to the Delta variant. The highly contagious new variant had already accounted for nearly 27 percent of all coronavirus strains recorded in the state as of June 19, according to the latest COVID-19 Variant Surveillance Report released by the NJ Department of Health and the CDC. We hosted Dr. David Adinaro, deputy commissioner of public health services for the New Jersey Department of Health, and our partners were able to ask him about the latest case numbers in the state, what residents needed to know about the variant, Delta variant symptoms, concerns about possible new lockdowns, and how people can keep their families and communities safe.

Our September press briefing had a different focus. After a number of discussions with our partners and reporting fellows, we decided to host a press briefing about [the impact the pandemic was having on immigrant communities](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/press-event-the-impacts-of-covid-19-on-immigrants-tickets-171273683197) in New Jersey. We invited Nedia Morsy, Director of Organizing at Make the Road NJ, and Muzaffar Chishti, Director of the MPI office at the New York University School of Law to answer questions about how people of color and immigrant families in NJ had been particularly impacted by the ongoing pandemic, including the state’s undocumented immigrant population.

_**Non-COVID press briefings for NJ media**_ In the wake of the horrific, racist shooting at a massage parlor in Atlanta, GA, the Center decided to dedicate our next press briefing in June to making sure our partners understood how to ensure [sensitivity, accuracy, and cultural nuance in their coverage of AAPI communities](https://www.eventbrite.com/e/sensitivity-accuracy-and-cultural-nuance-covering-aapi-communities-in-nj-tickets-157984268199) in NJ. As journalists covering communities in New Jersey, we believe it’s crucial to constantly reflect and look closely at our responsibility in covering issues related to race and ethnicity.

The briefing included Jun Choi, co-founder of Jersey Promise and former mayor of Edison, NJ; Jongwon Lee, reporter for Korean-language media and lawyer representing one of the victims’ families in the Atlanta shooting; and Brian Jon, founder of Asian American Youth Council in New Jersey. The discussion will be moderated by Mary Chao, reporter and columnist for The Record and NorthJersey.com.

In October, Oni Advincula — through sheer force of will — was able to [secure an exclusive press briefing](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V1Y9sZHa0F0) for NJ News Commons members (right before the gubernatorial election) with none other than NJ Gov. Phil Murphy. During the briefing, one of our members asked Murphy if New Jersey would follow in the footsteps of New York City and California and [set aside advertising dollars](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/will-murphy-set-aside-advertising-money-for-ethnic-media-in-new-jersey-12315f86c8a4) to spend specifically with ethnic and community media.

“I wouldn’t necessarily commit, but it’s something I would absolutely consider. We do some of this already,” said Murphy. “I would be very much open to doing that. It’s a smart way to get to people. And frankly, in a more targeted, closer to the ground way than the big, other media players, whether they be print radio, or television or digital.”

_**Profiles of local news and information providers**_ One of the last aspects of our reporting fellowship was a series of profiles highlighting two of our ethnic media partners in NJ. The first was a profile on how [a small, Korean weekly called Miju News](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/how-a-small-korean-newsroom-in-new-jersey-finds-a-way-to-serve-the-community-and-thrive-12ebe6f6d059), also known as the Korean Business Journal, is able to serve its niche community and thrive. With seven full-time reporters (five of them based in the paper’s headquarters in Fairfax, VA) and five freelancers, Miju News has been thriving by focusing on what many hemorrhaging mainstream news outlets cannot do: sticking to its unique audience, taking advantage of its language skills and culturally-nuanced knowledge about the community, and practicing advocacy journalism.

The second profile was a brief history of [how NJ-based Hungarian weekly Amerikai Népszava has survived](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/celebrating-130-years-this-nj-hungarian-weekly-has-endured-wars-and-pandemics-and-is-thriving-96f57f5d2704) the test of time for 130 years — through World War I and II, the Holocaust, the Great Depression, and global pandemics that ravaged humanity — and managed to thrive as a newly-digital publication.

“We now get up to 50,000 online visits a day — and it’s fascinating that they are from every corner of Hungary and America,” publisher Laszlo Bartus said. “Because of our expanded reach, we have been able to maintain an outsize impact.”

_**The NJ News Commons relaunches with new member tiers, benefits, and more**_ In December, we rolled out [a months-in-the-making relaunch and restructuring of the NJ News Commons](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/center-for-cooperative-media-relaunches-and-expands-nj-news-commons-its-flagship-project-600268fea72a), our flagship network of news and information providers from all across the state.

The NJ News Commons had previously limited its membership to news organizations and journalism freelancers in the past. But it became clear that many across our state are invested in news and information. Moreover, access to robust, accurate information can save lives and [supports civic health and community resiliency](https://www.usip.org/guiding-principles-stabilization-and-reconstruction-the-web-version/stable-governance/civic-particip).

To expand membership and better support local news in New Jersey, the Center **recreated the News Commons around an individual membership structure**.

By transitioning to a network structure that builds relationships with **individuals**—including journalists, media creators and concerned citizens — we sought to foster stronger partnerships with those directly involved in producing the news and content that inform NJ communities, as well as with local news readers and enthusiasts, and affiliated academic, community, and philanthropic institutions that round out [New Jersey’s news ecosystem](http://newsecosystems.org/).

[Click here to read a full write-up about the relaunch by Cassandra Etienne.](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/center-for-cooperative-media-relaunches-and-expands-nj-news-commons-its-flagship-project-600268fea72a)

## **2022: A decade of cooperative media – Growth, clarity, and new initiatives**

_**Marking 10 years with reflection and expansion**_ In 2022, the Center for Cooperative Media celebrated its 10th anniversary. Launched in 2012, the Center had reached a milestone and used the occasion for both reflection and a renewed push forward. “Earlier this semester, the Center… celebrated its 10th anniversary,” [Montclair State reported in November](https://www.montclair.edu/school-of-communication-and-media/2022/11/08/scms-center-of-cooperative-media-celebrates-10-years-of-shaping-new-jerseys-news-ecosystem/#:~:text=Earlier%20this%20semester%2C%20the%20Center,news%20industry%20in%20New%20Jersey).

To commemorate, the team implemented a 10-month historical project, sharing monthly stories from the Center’s decade of operations via its newsletter, and partnered with 10 New Jersey media leaders to publish forward-looking essays about the next decade of local news. The Center also hosted an in-person anniversary event honoring its founders, partners, and supporters, acknowledging their contributions to NJ’s media landscape.

Even as it looked back, the Center took concrete steps to expand its reach and membership. The new NJ News Commons individual membership model (launched late 2021) was in full swing, and the Center actively fundraised around its anniversary, aiming to raise $10,000 to further “expand access and foster connections” in NJ local news.

Director Murray noted that opening the NJ News Commons to anyone interested in NJ news was a key shift to get “more people interested and involved” in local journalism as active stakeholders.

_**Strengthening programs and funding**_ Programmatically, 2022 was a year of _robust activity with more clarity_, as the Center hit its stride after the chaos of the prior two years. Regular training resumed (or continued virtually/hybrid), including workshops on covering New Jersey’s midterm elections and on climate change and disaster preparedness for local reporters.

The Center hosted the annual Collaborative Journalism Summit in May 2022 as a hybrid event in Chicago (its first return to an in-person conference format since 2019). The South Jersey Information Equity Project (SJIEP), which began in 2020, progressed into new phases – by 2022, the Center had [a dedicated South Jersey Information Equity Project coordinator](https://njhumanities.org/njch-and-the-center-for-cooperative-media-launch-new-south-jersey-community-reporters-initiative/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=njch-and-the-center-for-cooperative-media-launch-new-south-jersey-community-reporters-initiative#:~:text=The%20New%20Jersey%20Council%20for,SJIEP) on staff and was [laying the groundwork](https://centerforcooperativemedia.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/2022-CCM-annual-report.pdf) to train community correspondents (as would happen in 2023)​.

Importantly, the Center’s funding base in 2022 included a mix of private and public streams: Montclair State University (operational support), Geraldine R. Dodge Foundation, Democracy Fund, the Abrams Foundation, and even the NJ Civic Information Consortium itself​. This marked the first time the NJ Civic Info Consortium provided a grant _to_ the Center – a reflection of the Center’s pivotal role in executing statewide media projects. (Earlier, the Center had only facilitated NJCIC’s work; now it was also a grantee, receiving funds to support its New Jersey media programs.) This diverse funding illustrates the Center’s nonpartisan yet public-interest orientation – it is backed by philanthropies dedicated to civic media as well as state-funded initiatives for community information.

Throughout 2022, the Center continued to champion collaborative journalism and media equity. It remained a key organizer of the national [U.S. Democracy Day](http://usdemocracyday.org/) project (launched in late 2022) – an effort where newsrooms across the country produce coverage on the state of American democracy each September 15. The Center served as a hub providing guidance and mini-grants for Democracy Day participants, reflecting its expanding influence beyond New Jersey.

On the research front, the Center published findings on the emergence of “permanent collaborative journalism” models in the U.S., and staff like Joe Amditis contributed to national conversations (e.g., via [Nieman Lab](https://www.niemanlab.org/2022/12/ai-throws-a-lifeline-to-local-publishers/#:~:text=AI%20throws%20a%20lifeline%20to,Nieman%20Lab%20asks%20some)) on innovations to support local news. Internally, the Center grew its team: for example, it welcomed membership coordinator Amanda Alicea to manage the Commons and assistant director of programming Cassandra Etienne to help run its many initiatives. By the end of 2022, the Center felt “clear on its mission” and was able to look back on “a decade of growth” with pride.

Montclair’s School of Communication and Media [praised the Center](https://www.montclair.edu/school-of-communication-and-media/2022/11/08/scms-center-of-cooperative-media-celebrates-10-years-of-shaping-new-jerseys-news-ecosystem/#:~:text=By%20using%20a%20unique%20collaborative,fundraising%20campaign%2C%20the%20Center%20hopes) for having “shaped the Garden State’s news landscape in unprecedented ways” through its collaborative approach.

Over ten years, the Center had hosted dozens of conferences, run 200+ training sessions, and funneled significant grant money into NJ journalism​ – tangible impacts on the local news infrastructure. With a strong foundation, the Center was poised to leverage its New Jersey successes for broader impact.

## **2023: Deepening local impact and building national presence**

In 2023, the Center for Cooperative Media moved from crisis response to strategic growth. After several tumultuous years, the focus shifted to implementing key facets of its multi-year strategic plan: enhancing diversity and inclusion internally, strengthening ties with New Jersey partners (especially in underserved areas), and increasing its national profile as an advocate for collaborative news​.

_**Local news ecosystem support**_ The Center doubled down on New Jersey initiatives in 2023, particularly in communities that had been historically overlooked by mainstream media. The South Jersey Information Equity Project (SJIEP) gained momentum – by this year, it had run multiple cohorts of reporting fellowships supporting journalists of color in South Jersey and partnered with community organizations.

In September 2023, the Center and the New Jersey Council for the Humanities launched the “ [South Jersey Community Reporters](https://njhumanities.org/njch-and-the-center-for-cooperative-media-launch-new-south-jersey-community-reporters-initiative/?utm_source=rss&utm_medium=rss&utm_campaign=njch-and-the-center-for-cooperative-media-launch-new-south-jersey-community-reporters-initiative#:~:text=The%20New%20Jersey%20Council%20for,SJIEP)” program, an innovative fellowship to train residents in Black communities across South Jersey as local reporters. Participants received funding (up to $5,100 each) and attended workshops on media literacy, data reporting, and storytelling, working alongside professional mentors like Tennyson Donyéa of _Black In Jersey_. The goal was to empower South Jerseyans to produce and share news that truly reflects their communities – blending grassroots journalism with public humanities.

This program demonstrates the Center’s community-driven philosophy and its effort to address news deserts _from the ground up_. It also exemplifies the Center’s collaborative approach to partnerships: NJCH, a state humanities nonprofit, provided support and a civic framework, while the Center contributed journalism expertise and project infrastructure. Together, they aimed to create a “better-represented and more equitable region” through local news.

Meanwhile, the Center continued regular services to the NJ media ecosystem. It hosted networking events and topical trainings for its NJ News Commons members and sustained bilingual news translation services, with dozens of stories rendered into Spanish (and other languages) to broaden audience reach​.

The Center’s [Local Connection Newsletter](https://us5.campaign-archive.com/home/?u=7f46611cb324e9e193acda7cc&id=7530e38571) and other communications highlighted opportunities, grants, and best practices to member newsrooms, keeping the collaborative network active. In 2023, the Center also presented the 6th annual [Excellence in NJ Local News Awards](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/winners-selected-for-2023-excellence-in-nj-local-news-awards-46fc0bb7ef4b#:~:text=Winners%20selected%20for%202023%20Excellence,in%20NJ%20Local%20News%20awards), honoring outstanding reporting by New Jersey journalists (including local online outlets like TAPinto)​. These awards spotlight critical civic journalism – for example, investigative stories on municipal governance and community development – and thus encourage high standards and accountability reporting in the state.

_**National initiatives and recognition**_ On the national stage, the Center’s reputation as a leader in collaborative journalism continued to grow in 2023. It co-hosted the Collaborative Journalism Summit (bringing together journalists from around the country to share collaboration case studies and skills), and its staff played a key role in organizing the [2023 U.S. Democracy Day](https://collaborativejournalism.org/democracyday/about/#:~:text=Journalism%20collaborativejournalism,democracy%20reporting) effort (the third annual). The Center provided coordination and resources that helped newsrooms in all 50 states participate in Democracy Day 2024 coverage of threats to democracy.

Additionally, the Center was laying the groundwork for a larger national program to support collaborative news (a plan that would come to fruition in 2024–25 with new funding). Research remained part of its portfolio: In late 2023, the Center published or contributed to reports on collaborative journalism’s efficacy and began exploring the intersection of civic science and journalism (it [secured a Civic Science Fellow](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/center-for-cooperative-media-welcomes-catherine-devine-as-2024-2025-civic-science-fellow-eef77a8f698f#:~:text=Center%20for%20Cooperative%20Media%20welcomes,by%20the%20Rita%20Allen%20Foundation) to start in 2024 with Rita Allen Foundation funding​). Staffing saw further growth and change: the Center [welcomed new team members](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/center-for-cooperative-media-welcomes-new-staff-members-a7fc266a8eb6#:~:text=Center%20for%20Cooperative%20Media%20welcomes,research%20assistant%20at%20the%20Center) like a full-time research coordinator to assist with its expanding research output. By the end of 2023, the Center reported that it had largely transitioned out of “turmoil” mode (pandemic/election crises) and refocused on its core objectives, making progress on equity, partnerships, and national advocacy for collaboration.

The stage was set for a major leap in 2024, backed by a new strategic roadmap and impending investment.

## 2024: Recommitment to New Jersey and a national leap forward

_**Focus on New Jersey civic information**_ In 2024, the Center continued “heads-down” work to strengthen New Jersey’s local news while simultaneously preparing to expand its collaborative model nationally. This year marked the culmination of the Center’s previous five-year strategic plan (2019–2024), prompting the team to draft a new roadmap for the future.

Key wins in NJ included deepening engagement in South Jersey, supporting ethnic media, and innovating new programs for community media. The Center hosted its first-ever New Jersey Ethnic & Community Media Expo in 2024 – a statewide event bringing together community journalists, ethnic press, and local news startups to share knowledge and resources (a capstone of its work to elevate ethnic and community media). It also reported providing expanded Spanish-language translation support to partners, reflecting the growing Latino population’s news needs.

Another highlight was the Center’s most successful U.S. Democracy Day to date in September 2024 – acting as a coordinator, it helped hundreds of newsrooms produce democracy-related stories, underscoring the Center’s commitment to pro-democracy journalism.

To bolster science-based reporting, the Center [welcomed Catherine Devine as its 2024–25 Civic Science Fellow](https://medium.com/centerforcooperativemedia/center-for-cooperative-media-welcomes-catherine-devine-as-2024-2025-civic-science-fellow-eef77a8f698f#:~:text=Center%20for%20Cooperative%20Media%20welcomes,by%20the%20Rita%20Allen%20Foundation) (funded by Rita Allen Foundation), embedding a scientist-journalist to foster collaborations that improve science and environmental news coverage.

One of the Center’s hallmark 2024 projects was a collaboration with _NJ Spotlight News_ to produce a statewide voter guide ahead of the 2024 elections. This nonpartisan voter guide compiled essential information on candidates and voting procedures and was distributed through local outlets across NJ – a practical example of the Center’s impact on keeping citizens informed. By coordinating such a project at scale, the Center filled information gaps that individual small newsrooms might not have had the capacity to tackle alone.

_**Funding and strategic transition**_ Financially, 2024 was relatively “quiet” but crucial behind the scenes. The Center maintained its core grant funding and university support and used the year to lay the groundwork for a major national initiative.

Knowing that its longtime New Jersey funders (like the Dodge Foundation) were shifting priorities – Dodge had announced plans to sunset its traditional journalism funding – the Center sought new investment to sustain and grow its mission. Those efforts paid off when, in late 2024, the Center secured a commitment from the Knight Foundation for a transformative grant (announced publicly in early 2025).

Internally, the team crafted a new strategic plan focusing on scaling their collaborative model beyond New Jersey while continuing to serve as a backbone for the NJ news ecosystem. By year’s end, the Center was poised for a _“renewed national effort”_ in support of collaborative journalism, even as it reaffirmed its dedication to local community information needs.

In essence, 2024 was a bridge year – strengthening home base operations in NJ (with projects like community media expo and local news translations) and readying the launch pad for national expansion.

## **2025: New horizons – A national hub for collaboration and current outlook**

As of early 2025, the Center for Cooperative Media stands at a pivotal moment, evolving from a primarily New Jersey-focused program into a national resource for collaborative journalism. In February 2025, Montclair State University announced that the Center had been awarded a $2.5 million grant from the Knight Foundation to expand collaborative journalism nationwide.

This five-year grant – the largest single funding in the Center’s history – enables the creation of a [Collaborative Journalism Resource Hub](http://collaborativejournalism.org/hub) to incubate and support journalism collaboratives across the United States. The Hub will back _20 collaboratives_ (about four new projects per year) with coaching, up to $50k in seed funding each, and resources to help them develop sustainable models of cooperation in local news.

In effect, the Center is leveraging its decade of expertise to formalize a national program that helps news organizations work together – whether it’s regional collaborations, topic-based networks, or community-centered media coalitions. Notably, this initiative builds on earlier work by the Solutions Journalism Network’s Local Media Project; veteran journalist Liza Gross, who led that project, has joined the Center’s new Hub as a senior adviser, and Amy Maestas came on as the Hub’s director. Their addition marks a significant staff expansion for 2025, bringing in high-profile talent to guide the Center’s national collaboration efforts.

Despite this broadened scope, the Center remains anchored in New Jersey and its original mission of serving the Garden State’s information needs. The NJ News Commons continues to connect hundreds of journalists and civic members statewide, facilitating content sharing and collaborative reporting on issues from municipal government to public health.

The Center’s research and data “holdings” – such as its comprehensive map of NJ news outlets, collaboration guidebooks, and case studies – are maintained as open resources via its websites (CenterforCooperativeMedia.org and CollaborativeJournalism.org).

Signature projects like Loved & Lost NJ live on as a testament to the power of collective storytelling (the online memorial is still growing as families submit names of loved ones lost to COVID-19). Ongoing initiatives in 2025 include the South Jersey Information Equity Project (now running its fourth cohort of fellows, plus the community reporters program) and annual events like the Collaborative Journalism Summit (scheduled to take place in spring 2025, sharing lessons from the new collaboratives).

The Center also retains its role as a key partner to the NJ Civic Information Consortium, helping ensure that state-funded media innovation grants translate into impactful community journalism.

In terms of staffing, the Center’s leadership has been consistent through 2020–2025: Stefanie Murray remains director (since 2016), guiding the Center’s vision, with long-time team members like Joe Amditis (assistant director) and Hanna Siemazsko (research director) contributing continuity and expertise. Around them, the staff has grown to include coordinators for membership, events, newsletters, and special projects (ethnic media, collaborative storytelling, etc.)​ – reflecting the Center’s multi-faceted operations.

This relatively small but dedicated team executes the Center’s programs with an ethos of “cooperation” and public service journalism. The Center’s ideological stance is evident in its work: while nonpartisan and academic in nature, it is deeply rooted in the belief that a healthy democracy requires an informed, engaged citizenry and a vibrant local press. Its programs prioritize civic issues, diversity in media voices, and community empowerment, aligning with a progressive vision of journalism as a tool for equity and social good​.

At the same time, it works across the political spectrum – the bipartisan support for the Civic Info Consortium and the Center’s collaborations with both small local outlets and major news organizations illustrate its practical, bridge-building approach.

_**Current holdings and impact**_ As of 2025, the Center for Cooperative Media can point to a rich portfolio of “holdings” in terms of programs, partnerships, and knowledge assets:

**— Networks:** A statewide NJ News Commons network (with nearly 200 individual members and dozens of outlet partners)​ and growing national networks through initiatives like Democracy Day and the new Knight-funded collaboratives. It also manages the NJ College News Commons, linking campus newsrooms, and facilitates ad-hoc networks (e.g., for pandemic coverage or elections) as needed.

**— Programs and resources:** Active programs include ongoing fellowships (supporting ethnic media, community reporters, and science journalists), annual events (Collaborative Journalism Summit, NJ media awards), and content-sharing projects (e.g., the Loved & Lost COVID memorial, statewide voter guides). The Center has produced practical guides and research reports on collaborative journalism that are freely available. It maintains an online New Jersey news ecosystem database and map for public reference, highlighting gaps and opportunities in local news.

**— Partnerships:** The Center has institutionalized partnerships with organizations such as _NJ Spotlight News_ (now NJ PBS) for co-production of content, the NJ Council for the Humanities for community training​, and national groups like the Solutions Journalism Network and Freedom Forum on special projects. Montclair State University remains a key partner, providing infrastructure and aligning the Center’s goals with broader educational missions. The Center’s collaborations span public entities (e.g., municipal governments for information outreach or state health departments for press briefings) and private foundations (Knight, Dodge, Democracy Fund, Rita Allen, etc.), exemplifying cross-sector cooperation.

**— Funding:** On the financial side, the Center’s holdings include multi-year grants that ensure stability (the Knight Foundation’s $2.5M grant, ongoing support from Democracy Fund and others) and a proven ability to attract project-based funding (e.g., [Google GNI](https://www.montclair.edu/newscenter/2021/12/02/google-funds-research-to-assist-news-startups/#:~:text=The%20project%20is%20supported%20by,develop%20new%20publishing%20business%20models), NJCIC grants​). This blend of public and private funding continues to sustain its operations and growth, insulating it (to some degree) from the market volatility that plagues commercial local media.

After 2012–2022’s focus on New Jersey, the period of 2020 to 2025 has seen the Center for Cooperative Media mature into both a statewide institution and a national influencer. It weathered an unprecedented pandemic by rallying collaborative solutions, helped steer public investment to where news needs are greatest, and celebrated a decade of impact with an eye toward the future. In the local news landscape of New Jersey, the Center’s influence is unmistakable – it has increased communication and resource-sharing among NJ outlets and uplifted voices (ethnic media, hyperlocals, community storytellers) that strengthen democracy at the grassroots.

Nationally, as it launches the Collaborative Journalism Resource Hub, the Center is poised to export its cooperative model, validating the notion that when journalists join forces, communities are better informed and better served. In sum, from 2020 through 2025 the Center for Cooperative Media evolved dramatically: guided by its founding mission and values, it responded to crises, innovated programs for equity in journalism, expanded partnerships and staff, and set the stage for a new era of collaborative journalism – all while remaining fundamentally committed to helping local news thrive in New Jersey and beyond.
